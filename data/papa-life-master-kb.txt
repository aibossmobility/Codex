FULL BIOGRAPHICAL NARRATIVE:

Early Life in Chicago

Brian Keith Hill was born in 1958 in Chicago, Illinois, and spent his
formative years on the west and south sides of the city. His mother
worked for the Chicago Public School District, a steady and respected
position that anchored the family economically. His father was a man of
many ambitions — he owned a store, worked for United Airlines, harbored
a passion for television camera work (the kind done behind the news desk,
not in front of it), and eventually settled into a long-term role with
the Chicago Public School District as well. The household was modest but
purposeful, and education was spoken of as the only reliable ladder out
of the circumstances that surrounded them.

From kindergarten through fifth grade, Brian attended Morris Elementary
School. He moved on to Morton Upper Grade Center for sixth through eighth
grade, then to Orr High School for his ninth year, and finally to Dunbar
Vocational High School from 1972 to 1976. These were the years of water
hydrants in the summer, of playing with friends in a neighborhood that
was simultaneously home and hazard. The family was among the first
African American families on their block on Sawyer Street — a distinction
that carried its own weight in a city still sorting itself out along
racial lines in the early 1960s. As children, they played together across
those lines without the overt racism that adults navigated. But the
community was underserved, financially strained, and marked by crime.
The uncertainty was constant.

Music was a refuge and a passion. Brian was a music major in high school,
and the Church of Christ was the spiritual center of family life. Both
would remain foundational throughout his entire story.

When Brian was approximately eleven years old, his father left the family
home. It was a rupture that sent a tremor through everything. "What life
was like is fear for me everywhere," he would later say. "Everywhere I
looked, it was internal, but nonetheless it was fear." That fear —
quiet, pervasive, internalized — became the engine of a decision that
would define the next decade of his life: I am going to leave this
community.

----------------------------------------------------------------

The Political World He Grew Up In

Brian came of age during one of the most turbulent periods in American
political history. Born in 1958, he was a child when John F. Kennedy was
assassinated, a young teenager when Robert F. Kennedy and Martin Luther
King Jr. were killed — three assassinations in a single decade that left
deep wounds in the African American community. The Voting Rights Act of
1964 had only just begun to open political doors, and the cost of that
legislation had been measured in lives.

In 1968, Chicago erupted in riots. The Black Panthers were active in both
California and Chicago. The Vietnam War was consuming young men from
underserved communities. Congress was, in the speaker's words, "all
white." Police were a source of danger rather than protection for Black
and brown people. Richard Nixon's presidency ended in impeachment. "I
just remember feeling afraid about everything," Brian recalls. "My
little young eyes and ears was taking in and seeing. It was really highly
charged."

He notes, with a clarity that carries no bitterness but considerable
weight, that the same conditions persist today: "The same things are
going on today. 60 years ago was going on then."

----------------------------------------------------------------

The Choice and the Departure

By the time Brian reached his late teens, the choice before him was
stark. "The choices I had in life before college was gangs and death or
ministry and education," he would later say. "I chose ministry and
education." In August 1976, at eighteen years old, he left Chicago for
Southwestern Christian College in Terrell, Texas. It was his first real
departure from Chicago, and it was permanent.

On the first day of school, he met Felice. She was seventeen. He was
eighteen. Nine months later, they were married. After college, Brian
served in the Army for six to seven months of training, spending 1977
and 1978 in service. He was discharged on March 23, 1978. Felice arrived
in Berkeley, California in January 1979. Brian followed in March. Their
daughter Simone was eight or nine months old. Their son Isaac would be
born in December of that same year. Brian was twenty years old. They had
almost nothing. They had each other. They had California.

----------------------------------------------------------------

Starting Over — Building a Family in the Bay Area

From 1979 to 1985, Brian enrolled in nine colleges pursuing a degree in
education: Laney College, Merritt College, College of Alameda,
Southwestern Christian College, and others. He worked as a weight
training fitness instructor at the Berkeley YMCA and Nautilus of Alameda.
He competed in the Mr. Oakland bodybuilding contest and placed second.
He pursued racquetball with enough intensity to go professional. He sang.

In 1981, Brian and Felice moved to Vacaville, California, to work at
Sierra Children's Home, where they took in eight children who were wards
of the state. At twenty-one and twenty-two years old, they were parenting
ten children — their own two plus eight others.

In 1982-1983, they moved to Valparaiso, Indiana, to work at another
children's home. Then came a job offer at Crow Canyon Athletic Club in
San Ramon, California. Brian and Felice packed everything they owned into
the car and drove from Indiana to California. He arrived for the in-
person interview. The woman told him he did not have the job.

"I remember walking to the car from the Crow Canyon Athletic Club and
having to tell Felice, I don't have a job. We have all our belongings in
the car. We drove six, seven, eight days to get there, and the next thing
I know, I don't have a job. I remember just crying, trying to figure out
what are we going to do now. That was very hard. Very hard."

----------------------------------------------------------------

The Values That Carried Him Through

Four words remained constant throughout all of it: giving, listening,
loving, and serving. "Just like the sun, the moon, the stars, the water,
the fire, it never goes out," Brian says. "They always do those four
things."

Faith was not incidental. The Church of Christ, the Bible, and a belief
that God was the reason he arrived where he did are woven throughout
every recording. "I really realized God was the one that allowed us to
come where we had come."

----------------------------------------------------------------

Legacy, Roots, and the Gift of Memory

Brian and Felice raised Simone and Isaac in California. In 2017, Brian
learned that the Hill family homestead traces back nine generations —
to Lakeland, Florida and Decatur, Georgia. Before Chicago, before
California, there was a family in the South.

In the Socially recording, he speaks directly to his grandchild Jacoby:
"Since mom and dad were born in Berkeley and in Chicago, now that we've
grown up and you're growing up, you're looking back and seeing there is
really a 60 to 90 year history that you never knew about, but you're
learning some of it now."

On May 21, 2026 — the forty-ninth wedding anniversary of Brian and
Felice Hill — these six audio recordings were made. They are a
transmission: one man's deliberate act of giving the next generation the
map he had to draw himself, one decision at a time, from the west side
of Chicago to the shores of California.

and education. I chose ministry and education."
— Brian Keith Hill
